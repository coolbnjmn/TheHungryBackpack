June 25th, 2015

Day 1 in Dublin

With its incredible food, beer, and more than friendly people, it is really hard not to fall in love with Dublin. The city center itself is quaint and vibrantly historical, with cobblestone streets, renovated tenement buildings, pubs, shops, and music overflowing all over the place. Magically landing accommodations in the Trinity College dorms has allowed us to be engulfed in a youthful atmosphere with the freedom to explore Dublin directly from the center of the city. Everything is at our fingertips! Naturally, our first day here consisted of exploration and lots of eating. Jet lagged and starving, we walked right over to Hatch & Sons for a late breakfast. The small below-street-level cafe served us smoked salmon, sausage, eggs, bacon, and soda bread in a charming 18th century room (at an even more charming price *corny elbow jab*).
Following breakfast was a stroll through Saint Stephens Park, which turned into a marathon walk to various markets and shops, then Dublin Castle, and finally the Trinity College library, where we stared wide-eyed at the Book of Kells (est. 800 AD) and two stories worth of centuries old books. By the time dinner time rolled around, we had walked approximately 9 miles throughout the day, earning us our first Guinness of the trip. We stopped in a pub near The Temple Bar district, and downed our Guinness with a large corned beef & soda bread plate (do we sound like tourists yet? Sorry, not sorry) all while a man played guitar and sang about a lover in Kilkenny as passersby clapped along. Summer days in Ireland last forever, as the sun rises at 5am and sets at a whopping 11pm! Our bodies are very confused.

June 26th, 2015

A day in Northern Ireland

As I write this, we are passing what feels like our ten thousandth sheep and cow filled field (and no, I am definitely not complaining.) Driving three hours up the Eastern coast of Ireland has proven that the photo of baby sheep frolicking amongst lush green Irish landscapes that you've probably seen in a calendar once or twice is REAL. It's very real and very breathtaking. There's something about the fact that you can pass a gas station, and then the ruins of a 15th century castle with sheep running around it that blows your mind more than you could ever know. Anyway, our first stop on our day trip up to Northern Ireland was the Dark Hedges in Ballymoney. The Dark Hedges are used in Game of Thrones as "The Kingsroad" and are an eerily beautiful avenue of gigantic beech trees planted over 300 years ago. From there, we drove on and eventually headed onto the Carrick-a-Rede rope bridge to Carrick Island. Suspended 100 ft above sea level, you would think we would be crying while crossing a 350 year old bridge made of ropes and wooden planks that could give way any moment, but the views were so insane that they distracted you from thinking about anything else. Well, that and one of the bridge attendants who talked with us for a while about Limp Bizkit (random) and the Freedom Tower in New York (Thanks Emmett). After surviving the bridge, taking some pretty cool photos (if I do say so myself), and getting yelled at by a sheep, we headed onward to the Giants Causeway.
The geological formation created some 50 million years ago due to volcanic eruption looks so calculated and perfect that it's kind of hard NOT to believe the myth of a giant being responsible for the landscape. Each cylindrical stone fits into one another like pegs or steps, making it easy to climb and explore (watch out for the wet ones after it rains or you'll slip into the Irish Sea, which we somewhat did...oops). Before heading to our final stop, we pulled over to look at another landscape used in Game of Thrones. These vast castle ruins, called Shane's Castle, hung over an oceanside cliff with a whole clan of cows comfortably surrounding it. The castle is used for exterior shots of both 'Castle Black' and 'Winterfell'...so safe to say we fangirled pretty hard all day. Finally, we made it to our last stop of the day, the city of Belfast. We sat in the park of City Hall, where there stood a memorial for those lost on the Titanic, the Belfast-made ship which they undoubtedly have a lot of pride for, coining the phrase "Made by Irishman, sunk by Englishman." UPDATE: After heading back down the coast and topping off the night with dinner and drinks in Dublin's VERY fun Porterhouse, we lay in bed burping up strawberry beers, oysters, and a bacon loin? Our feet are sore and somewhat raw from walking a collective 7 miles, but our smiles are wide and our bellies are happy.
